Is the watermark copyrighted?

The answer turns on what the mark legally is. Plain-language overview, not legal advice.

Two things people call a “watermark”

  • A copyright/authorship watermark — a photographer's name, a stock-site logo. It identifies the rights holder.
  • A provenance watermark — a mark stating how content was made (e.g. AI), not who owns it. The Gemini sparkle is this kind.

What DMCA §1202 protects

17 U.S.C. §1202 makes it illegal to strip copyright management information — title, author, copyright notice — in order to hide or misrepresent authorship. Its purpose is to stop passing off someone else's work by scrubbing credits. The Gemini sparkle doesn't identify an author or assert copyright; it says "this was made with an AI tool." So a colorable argument is that §1202 — aimed at authorship attribution — isn't squarely aimed at AI-provenance marks. The §1202 case law is almost all about photographer credits and stock metadata, not AI stamps.

So is removing it legal?

Removing a cosmetic provenance mark from an image you have the rights to is generally not prohibited by §1202 — you're already the author, so you're not concealing authorship. The bigger question is almost always the image itself, not the mark:

  • You generated it with Gemini: you generally own the output and can edit it (subject to Google's terms and local rules).
  • Someone else's image: removing the mark gives you no rights to the underlying image. Copyright in the image is separate from the mark on it.

Rule of thumb: only remove the sparkle from images you own or are licensed to use. Removing a mark never creates rights you didn't already have.

Disclosure can matter more than the law

Where removal is lawful, disclosure rules can still bite. Platforms and jurisdictions increasingly require AI-content labels. Strip the mark and publish without disclosing, and the problem is the missing disclosure, not pixel-editing. Removing the corner star for design reasons while still labelling the image as AI-made is very different from passing it off as non-AI.

SynthID

Gemini images also carry invisible SynthID — an imperceptible statistical signature built to survive editing for later identification. It's a provenance/anti-misuse tool, not a copyright tool. Our tool removes only the visible sparkle and does not touch SynthID. Trying to defeat SynthID is a different, adversarial category of activity.

The takeaway

  1. The sparkle is a provenance indicator, not a copyright notice.
  2. Removing it isn't generally a §1202 problem when you own the image.
  3. Removal grants no rights to the image — and disclosure rules may still apply.
  4. Only edit images you have rights to; be honest about AI provenance where required.
