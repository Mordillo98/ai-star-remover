How to remove the sparkle

An alpha-blended corner stamp, a fixed star, a reversible equation. The laziest guide that fully works — free, private, no upload. Updated June 2026.

What the watermark is

The Gemini sparkle isn't a sticker. It's an alpha blend — a fixed star design blended into the corner in pure white, governed by a known transparency map. Each affected pixel is a mix of white and the original underneath. Because the map is fixed and public, the blend is mathematically reversible: you recover the exact original pixel.

The math, in one line

watermarked = α·255 + (1−α)·original  →  original = (watermarked − α·255) / (1−α)

Apply that to every pixel in the corner patch and the star is gone. Two sizes exist — a 48px star for images up to 1024², a 96px star for larger, each with its own margin. The maps are MIT-licensed and open.

Steps

  1. Open the tool. Home page — nothing to install, no account.
  2. Drop your image onto the upload box, or click to choose. PNG, JPG, WEBP.
  3. Press Remove. Processing is entirely in your browser — the image is never sent anywhere.
  4. Download the cleaned PNG.

Privacy is structural here. There's no backend, so there's nothing to store. The image lives in browser memory and auto-clears five minutes after processing (or when you click Clear). No cookies of ours, no logs, no copies.

Which images it works on

  • Any image Gemini stamps with its visible sparkle, any resolution.
  • Best on PNG / lossless. Heavy JPEG adds noise that slightly reduces precision — export as PNG first if you can.
  • If the image was resized after generation the star may shift; work at the original size.

What it does not remove

Only the visible sparkle. Google also embeds an invisible SynthID signature — imperceptible, designed to survive editing. There is no legitimate way to remove SynthID, and this tool doesn't try. Removing a cosmetic stamp for design reasons is very different from defeating provenance detection.

Is it legal?

The sparkle is an AI provenance indicator, not a copyright notice, so removing it isn't the same as stripping a photographer's credit (which DMCA §1202 protects in the US).
